Introduction

Storage Management refers to the process of efficiently storing, organizing, and protecting digital data.

Redundancy means creating backup or duplicate copies of data to ensure availability and reliability.

As data grows rapidly in the digital era, managing and protecting storage systems has become crucial.

  • Understand RAID levels and their functions.
  • Learn about Logical Volume Management (LVM).
  • Compare NAS and SAN storage systems.

RAID: Redundant Array of Independent Disks

RAID combines multiple hard drives into one logical unit to offer data protection and performance improvement. Common levels include RAID 0, 1, 5, and 10.

RAID 0 – Striping

  • Splits data across multiple drives for speed.
  • No redundancy — one failure loses all data.
  • Best for non-critical high-speed tasks.

RAID 1 – Mirroring

  • Duplicates data on two or more drives.
  • Provides excellent protection.
  • Half storage is used for duplication.

RAID 5 – Striping with Parity

  • Balances speed, efficiency, and redundancy.
  • Can survive one drive failure.
  • Best for business servers.

RAID 10 – Mirroring + Striping

  • Combines RAID 0 and RAID 1.
  • High speed and strong redundancy.
  • Needs at least 4 drives; more expensive.

Logical Volume Management (LVM)

LVM allows dynamic disk management without downtime. It consists of:

  • PV (Physical Volume): Actual disks.
  • VG (Volume Group): Combined storage pool.
  • LV (Logical Volume): Virtual partitions for use.

LVM provides flexibility, scalability, and easier backups, making it ideal for large and growing data systems.

Conclusion

Storage management ensures data is organized, protected, and accessible, while redundancy safeguards against data loss. Technologies like RAID, LVM, NAS, and SAN make systems faster, safer, and more reliable.

Effective data management = Reliability + Performance + Security
